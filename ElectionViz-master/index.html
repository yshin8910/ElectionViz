The Great Recession and the 2008 Presidential Election

Home ownership had been expanding in the early 2000s and a housing boom was inevitable thanks to the Bush administrations legislature that allowed Americans to secure a low-interest federal loan to help in buying a house. This quickly back-fired as many Americans started to buy a house using loans that they couldn't afford to pay back. This led to what is known as the Great Recession in the United States. How the Recession Impacted the 2008 Election

During George W. Bush’s presidency, America fell into the Great Recession, the longest period of economic decline since the Great Depression in the 1930s. People were losing their jobs, savings, and homes while major banks were going bankrupt one after another.

President Bush and the U.S. Federal Reserve lowered the target interest rate, at one point to zero, and sent stimulus packages to encourage spending and get the economy up again, but the recession was already too deep in troubles for these actions to make a big impact. This recession caused a shift between voters of the two parties in America.

Differences in the 2004 and 2008 Presidential Election Turnout Between Parties

The choropleth map on the right displays the percentage change in the number of votes for the democratic and republican party in each state and county for the 2004 and 2008 general elections.

You can interact with the map by hovering over each state to see summary statistics about the data. Additionally, you can click on any state to zoom in and see the county-level data with the same hovering functionality.

A legend for the colors is display below. High blue coloring indicates a high change in democratic vote and a low change in republican vote from 2004 to 2008. Similarly, a high red coloring indicates a high change in republican vote and a low change in democratic vote. States which are colored maroon had a high change in both democratic and republican votes. Finally, a light coloring indicates a low change in the vote for both parties.

Republican Voters Shift to the Democratic Party

During the 2004 Presidential Election, the vote difference between presidential candidates George W. Bush and John F. Kerry was fairly even with Bush receiving 286 electoral votes and 62,040,610 popular votes and Kerry receiving 251 electoral votes and 59,028,444 popular votes.

In the 2008 presedential election, Barack Obama was able to capture the vote of many that were once a part of the Republican party. For example, it can be seen from the map that the Democratic party saw a 15.9% vote increase in Michigan from 2004 while the Republican party had a 11.5% vote decrease from 2004. Overall, Obama won the 2008 election with 39% Democrat voters and 32% Republican voters.

The response to the Great Recession from President Bush and the Republican party was a likely cause for many Republicans to vote for Obama instead of McCain because President Bush’s interventions by providing the stimulus package and lowering the target interest rate did not make a large impact on helping the economy and the American people.

Information sourced from 270 to Win (2004), 270 to Win (2008) and Pew Research Center. Data sourced from class datasets.

Michigan's Great Recession

This map shows the unemployment rate for each county in Michigan. You can interact with the map by hovering over a county to see summary statistics about the unemployment rate and the outcome of the 2008 election in that county.

During the Great Recession, Michigan was impacted more than other states because not only was it impacted by the general subprime mortgage crisis, where home loans were granted to those with poor credit history, but also it’s economy. Eventually, the bankruptcy of the automotive companies based in Michigan such as GM and Chrysler caused additional damage to Michigan's economy.

Michigan reached its peak unemployment rate of 10%, and compared to other states, the majority of the counties had a 9-10% unemployment rate. The majority of these counties saw a high turnout for Obama in the 2008 election.

Data sourced from the Bureau of Labor Statistics.

Florida's Great Recession

This map shows the unemployment rate for each county in Florida. You can interact with the map by hovering over a county to see summary statistics about the unemployment rate and the outcome of the 2008 election in that county.

One of the key swing states that favored Obama in 2008 was Florida. Florida also happened to be another one of the states which was hit hardest by the Great Recession. In particular, the areas hit hardest are in the southern regions of the state such as Palm Beach County and Hendry County which had high unemployment rates in 2008. Obama was able to campaign on the economic and employment hardships that the people of Florida were facing because of the recession and the turnout in population-dense counties which saw high unemployment favored Obama over McCain.

Data sourced from the Bureau of Labor Statistics.

African American Voters Turnout in 2008

This chart is plotting the voting rate of certain demographics in the United States over time. You can also filter some of the data points by toggling the line on or off by clicking on the corresponding checkbox at the top.

While unemployment and overall economic health was a large factor which contributed to the voting shift in 2008, there was an entire voting demographic which saw an incredible surge in 2008. As seen from this chart, the African American vote was on a steady upwards trend prior to the 2008 election but saw a surge in the 2008 election. These voters turned out overwhelmingly for Obama in 2008 helping contribute to his decisive victory over John McCain. Find out more information here.
